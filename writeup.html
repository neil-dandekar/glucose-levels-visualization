Unraveling Glucose Patterns: A 10-Day Look at Diabetes & Health

The Avengers: Neil Dandekar, Avi Mehta, Kevin Souder, Idhant Kumar

Introduction

Our team, The Avengers, chose to dive deeper into the CGMacros Dataset from the PhysioNet Databases. All three of us have a close relative or friend who has struggled with diabetes. Therefore, we felt compelled to make a visualization which allowed researchers to explore data more easily, which may help them make better and cheaper solutions for those with the disease.

In particular, we explored how the daily average glucose level fluctuated throughout the 10-day period and across all 45 participants in the study. Diabetes is a disease which affects glucose metabolism, therefore, analyzing glucose fluctuations over time was key to our visualization. Our goal is to answer questions regarding which factors may affect these fluctuations. with different diabetes status differs from one another throughout a 10 day study. Our take includes a bee swarm visualization, where each “bee” or blob represents an individual participant’s glucose level. Participants are also grouped by either non-diabetic, diabetic, or type 2 diabetic. We also incorporated a slider to view the data for each day of the study, with an option to animate the slider to loop through the day's automatically. In addition, The relative size of a blob represents the participant’s BMI and its shade of blue represents their microbiome gut health, where lighter blue is healthier. Overall, the chart is visually appealing, fluid, and easy to digest. Furthermore, it allows for easy analysis of the dataset.

Design Rationale

We considered multiple options originally. From line plots, to bar charts, to a visualization of the human body, we weren’t convinced that these would do the data justice. We wanted something more fluid as the goal was to make an interactive visualization.

After researching online, we came across some example bee swarm charts which we immediately started to play with. As the time series glucose data was the center of attention, we based the main axes around this data. Furthermore, it only made sense to use categorical data on the x-axis. We chose the patients’ diabetes status for the groupings because we assumed that trends within each group were most important for studying.

Another benefit to the bee swarm is the option to encode additional variables. For example, we used blob size and color to encode BMI and gut microbiome health. We decided that the visual size of a blob directly corresponds to a physical attribute like BMI which is, in a sense, related to the physical size of participants. We also wanted to indicate other health attributes about patients and realized that gut health may greatly affect one’s metabolism and glucose levels. Health is easy to categorize by color, so we chose a color encoding for this variable. We used a blue, monochromatic scale because it’s easy to interpret, and easy on the eyes. To help interpret these encodings, we used visual size scales for the BMI size legend and color labels for the gut health. With multiple displayed sizes for the BMI chart, the user is able to more easily interpret the blob sizes. However, to view exact data, as well as more attributes about individual participants (age, gender, etc.), users can hover over blobs for a tooltip of info.

Perhaps our most interactive element is the slider. The time series portion of this data was key and we loved the idea of being able to animate our visualization to display trends over time. Since time is a linear scale, we thought of a horizontal slider for the user to select different days of the study to display. Additionally, we added an “Animate” button to bring the data to life and automatically move throughout time. This utilized JavaScript’s setInterval() function that animated the Day Slider in a continuous loop, and could be stopped at the user’s discretion. By stopping at discrete days, users can make more in-depth observations for each day, and by animating, the user can get a better sense of the fluctuation trends over time. To make the blobs more fluid, we used the D3 force library to make the blobs bounce around and slide between each other.

Development Process and Time Allocation

Initially, our group met in person to brainstorm datasets to visualize, which charts to use, and ultimately settled on our design choices. We also did some introductory Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) on a single computer together to draft some initial plots and ideas.

Idhant spliced together some of the different csv files in the database to create a combined overview of data, including stats about each participant, in addition to the glucose data. Neil used python scripts to concatenate, clean, and merge all 45 individual participant files into one large dataset, which was also grouped by individual day in the study and the mean glucose level was recorded as separate columns to be displayed.

All of us were new to D3.js so it took some time getting familiar with. We spent some time also reviewing the documentation and watching YouTube videos to get a grasp.

As time went on, we distributed the programming tasks in parallel. After initializing a GitHub repository, each of us created our own branches to work on separate features. We developed the chart entirely in D3, combining HTML, CSS, and JavaScript to combine the design, content, layout, and interaction.

Overall, the tasks and time were split as followed (total: ~26 hours):

  • 1. Brainstorming and planning (all members, ~5 hours)
  • 2. Learning D3.js (all members, ~2 hours)
  • 3. Finalizing visualization design rationale (Avi & Kevin, ~2 hours)
  • 4. EDA (Idhant, Kevin, & Neil, ~6 hours)
    • Kevin: initial EDA charts
    • Idhant: combining individual participant attributes with glucose data
    • Neil: time series glucose data aggregation
  • 5. Programming (Avi, Kevin, & Neil, ~11 hours)
    • Various tasks split between all from designing the scales, axes, titles, colors, legend, and slider (+animation)
